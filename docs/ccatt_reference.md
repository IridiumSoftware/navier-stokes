## Definition (as given)

CCATT is a **constraint-first framework for evaluating the admissibility** of structures, primitives,
and theories. Central thesis: **existence is open** (globally unconstrained), while finite systems
achieve knowledge and closure only through **costed, curved, and bounded access**.

A claim, object, or theory is **admissible** only when it satisfies explicit governance conditions on:

- **Constraint** — the active constraint field that defines the problem.
- **Access** — finite, declared, bounded modes of reaching structure (construction, recognition,
  verification, repair, …).
- **Transport** — certified movement of invariants across representations / domains / scales /
  theaters, with an **explicit loss ledger** and **bridge validation**.
- **Certification** — a bounded interface that checks constraint satisfaction **without smuggling
  hidden primitives**.
- **Closure** — local, recoverable stabilization that does **not** claim global totality.

**Key principles.** No global closure is assumed or licensed. Primitives must demonstrate
**transition-irreducibility** and survive **selection-theater decontamination**. Problem *frames*
are themselves subject to an admissibility audit before solutions are evaluated. Dominance, predictive
success, elegance, or institutional survival do **not** license a primitive or frame — only audited
transition-necessity and decontamination do.

**Operational form.** A governance layer that filters candidate generator classes *after* subtracting
representational, instrumental, computational, and institutional artifacts. It **inverts the usual
authorization direction**: admissible generator classes *explain* observed exports, rather than exports
*authorizing* primitives. Applies uniformly across mathematical, physical, and epistemic domains.
